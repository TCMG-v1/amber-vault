## 1. Recitals & Purpose

This document is the **master trademark boilerplate** for the marks identified in Schedule A (collectively, the "Marks"). It is intended to:

(a) state the applicant entity, ownership chain, and first-use facts for each Mark uniformly across filings;
(b) preserve a single, signed source of truth that may be incorporated by reference into USPTO TEAS forms, foreign filings (Madrid Protocol, etc.), license agreements, assignments, and litigation pleadings;
(c) document the goods and services for which each Mark is or will be used, by Nice Classification class;
(d) memorialize the use-in-commerce specimens, dates of first use, and bona fide intent-to-use where applicable; and
(e) establish the quality-control mechanism required to preserve trademark rights under licensing.

This boilerplate is incorporated by reference into the IP Assignment Agreement (Document 04), the Brand License Agreement template (Document 05), and the Intercompany License Memorandum (Document 06).

## 2. Definitions

- **"Mark"** — any one of the trademarks, service marks, design marks, or trade dress identified in Schedule A.
- **"Applicant"** or **"Owner"** — the LLC identified in Schedule A as the registered owner of the Mark.
- **"First Use Date"** — the date on which the Mark was first used in the ordinary course of trade in connection with the identified goods/services.
- **"First Use in Commerce Date"** — the date on which the Mark was first used in interstate commerce within the meaning of 15 U.S.C. § 1127.
- **"Specimen"** — the example of use submitted to the USPTO demonstrating actual use of the Mark in connection with the goods/services.
- **"Nice Class"** — the international classification of goods and services per the Nice Agreement, as adopted by the USPTO.
- **"Licensee"** — any third party authorized in writing by Owner to use a Mark.
- **"Quality Standards"** — the standards set forth in the applicable Style Guide and any written approvals issued by Owner.

## 7. Quality Control & Licensing (Naked Licensing Prevention)

A trademark owner that licenses its mark without exercising adequate quality control may be deemed to have abandoned the mark under the doctrine of **naked licensing.** To prevent this, Owner shall, with respect to each Mark and each Licensee:

(a) require the Licensee to use the Mark only in conformity with the Style Guide applicable to that Mark;
(b) reserve and exercise the right to approve, in writing and in advance, every product, packaging, advertising, and promotional use of the Mark by Licensee, with samples submitted no fewer than fourteen (14) days before production;
(c) inspect, or cause to be inspected, Licensee's facilities and goods bearing the Mark not less than annually;
(d) retain the right to terminate the license upon Licensee's failure to maintain Quality Standards, with cure period not to exceed thirty (30) days;
(e) document all approvals, inspections, and corrective actions in a contemporaneous record;
(f) require Licensee to use the appropriate notice (™ for unregistered, ® for registered) in all uses of the Mark; and
(g) prohibit Licensee from challenging the validity of the Mark or Owner's ownership thereof during the term and for a reasonable period thereafter.

The foregoing provisions shall be incorporated, mutatis mutandis, into every license agreement involving any Mark.

## 8. Notice and Marking

(a) For registered Marks, Owner and its Licensees shall use the federal registration symbol ® adjacent to the Mark in each prominent use.

(b) For unregistered Marks (including those pending registration), Owner and its Licensees shall use ™ for trademarks and ℠ for service marks.

(c) A standard notice of ownership shall appear in legible form on packaging, websites, and significant promotional materials, in substantially the following form:

> "[MARK]® is a registered trademark of [BNS LLC | Agriparrium LLC]. All rights reserved. © [Year] [Owner]."

## 9. Enforcement Policy

Owner shall maintain a **Watch List** of confusingly similar marks and shall act on credible third-party infringement within a reasonable time, including by means of cease-and-desist correspondence, USPTO opposition or cancellation proceedings, UDRP filings for domain disputes, and civil litigation under the Lanham Act where warranted. Failure to enforce may weaken the Marks; conversely, over-enforcement against fair, comparative, or expressive uses is disfavored and shall be considered case-by-case in consultation with counsel.
