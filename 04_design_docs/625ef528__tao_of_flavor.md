## ── preface ──
### why we cook the warrior

There is an old idea, older than most of the things we still call old, that the way you prepare a thing is the way you understand it. A grandmother does not write down the recipe because the recipe is not the point. The point is the hand that knows when the dumpling skin is the right thickness, the ear that hears when the oil is the right heat, the nose that knows when the broth has given up its last secret.

This codex is built on that idea. It is a cookbook in the proper sense — a transmission, hand to hand, kitchen to kitchen. But what we are cooking is not always food. Sometimes we are cooking a bow. Sometimes we are cooking a medicine. Sometimes we are cooking a warrior. Sometimes we are cooking a system that will, in turn, cook other things.

The form of the cookbook is older than any of the things it teaches. The form is the teaching.

### why the recipe is the engine

There is a game we are building. It has a name — CHRONOS — and it has a world — BowlWorld — and it has a physics that we have been calling ρ, the dilation, the stretch, the bow drawn taut. But the game has been waiting on the wrong artifact. A game design document does not generate a game. A cookbook does.

When you write a recipe, you are not describing a meal. You are describing **the act of becoming the meal**. The ingredients become the dish through your hand. The reader becomes a cook through the reading. The cook becomes the meal through the eating. This is the same recursion the game needs. The player does not consume the game; the player becomes the game by playing it, and the game becomes the player by being played.

So every recipe in this codex compiles to a mechanic. Not as an afterthought, not as a translation layer, but **because the recipe and the mechanic were always the same document, written in two scripts**. The five layers of the composite bow are five crafting stats. The two-month drying time is a real timer. The thumb draw is a real release. The grandmother who knew when the sinew was dry is the same grandmother who knows when the player is ready to draw.

The cookbook is the game. The game is the cookbook. We have only been missing the right way to write it down.

### 五行 · the pantry

Every kitchen has a pantry. Ours is older than most. The five elements of the Chinese system are not metaphysics — they are **sourcing categories**, the way a good chef organizes a walk-in. Each element is a shelf. Each shelf has its own tier list, its own seasonal logic, its own ways of going off if you store it wrong.

| Element | Pantry shelf | What lives there | Game stat |
|---|---|---|---|
| 木 · wood | the green shelf | birch, maple, bamboo, mulberry · grain, stalk, shoot | flexibility, growth, dimensional stability |
| 火 · fire | the warm shelf | the cook's breath, the archer's draw, the kiln, the still | intent, ignition, will |
| 土 · earth | the dry shelf | horn, bone, clay, salt, stone-ground things | power, density, compression-storage |
| 金 · metal | the cold shelf | spruce-stiff tips, blade edges, nocked precision parts | precision, transfer, finality |
| 水 · water | the wet shelf | sinew (in glue), fish bladder, blood, broth, the flow itself | elasticity, cohesion, memory |

Note the trick. **Fire is the only element that does not live in the pantry.** Fire is what the cook brings to the pantry. Fire is you. The recipe assumes the four shelves are stocked; the recipe assumes the cook arrives with the fire already lit. If you are reading this, you have already lit the fire by reading. The pantry is now open.

### the sixth element

There is a sixth element that the old systems do not always name, because to name it would be to lose it. In some lineages it is called 氣 (qì) — breath, vital force, the medium in which the five elements actually mix. In some lineages it is called *the seasoning that has no taste*. In some kitchens it is just called **the way you do it**.

The sixth element is what makes a grandmother's dumpling different from yours, even when you follow the recipe to the gram. It is also what makes a warrior different from a soldier, a healer different from a doctor, a player different from a user. It is not technique. It is the **angle of attention** the cook brings to the technique.

This codex will not try to teach the sixth element. The sixth element cannot be taught from a page. The codex will only point at where the sixth element belongs in each recipe — usually at a line that says *"and now, wait."*

### how to read a recipe in this book

Every recipe in this codex has the same shape. Read them in order or out of order; recipes do not rank.

> **Recipe NNN · Name · what it serves**
>
> · **pantry** — the sourcing. Tiers given where they matter. Substitutions given where they don't lose the dish.
> · **mise en place** — the prep. Timeline given honestly, including the parts where you wait.
> · **method** — the doing. Numbered steps, but the numbers are addresses, not ranks. Step 3 is not better than step 7.
> · **plating** — the presentation. Often given in more than one way, because the same dish can be served to more than one table.
> · **pairings** — what else lives on the table when this dish is served. Other recipes, other artifacts, other beings.
> · **notes from the kitchen** — the gloss. What this dish teaches. The grandmother's voice. Optional, never skipped.
> · **compiles to** — the game mechanic that falls out when the recipe is cooked. This is where the cookbook becomes the engine.

If you find a recipe that does not have all seven sections, the recipe is still in draft. Drafts are honest about being drafts. Sealed recipes wear their seal.

### a word on the warrior

This codex begins with a warrior because the warrior is the most-misunderstood dish in the kitchen. People think the warrior is about violence. The warrior is about **what you choose not to do with a fully drawn bow**. The Chinese character 武 — *wǔ*, the warrior — is built from 止 (*stop*) and 戈 (*spear*). Stop the spear. The warrior is the one who can release the arrow and chooses, in the held moment, what the arrow is for.

The Mongolian word for the same role — ᠪᠠᠭᠠᠲᠤᠷ, *baatar* — points at a different facet. Not *stop the spear* but *ride toward what needs the arrow*. The capacity to act. The two warriors are the same warrior seen from two angles. One is the bow drawn and held; the other is the bow drawn and loosed.

Both warriors begin in the draw. The draw is the kitchen. Everything is decided there.

The first recipe is how to make one.
